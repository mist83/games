# Muay ThAI

- Built-in 3-distance Muay Thai game; keep `game.js` platform-loadable with `var GameLogic = ...`.
- Source of truth for strike ranges is `MOVES[*].range` in `game.js`.
- Keep the legal attack roster to one Light and one Heavy attack per range: distance `teep`/`flying_knee`, pocket `jab`/`cross`, clinch `elbow`/`knee`.
- Rules state intentionally has no guard, meter, long-lived resource, supers, lines, projectiles, side-switching, pivots-behind, or throws; do not reintroduce UI/renderer branches for mechanics absent from `MOVES`.
- Resolve movement before attacks; strike legality is checked against the post-movement range.
- Source of truth for spacing is `state.feet`: P1 starts at -1, P2 starts at 1; gap 0 is Clinch, gap 1 is Pocket, and gap 2 is capped Distance.
- Visual spacing must preserve the fight grammar: gap 0 overlaps front feet and lead hands for Clinch, gap 1 is toe-to-toe Pocket, and gap 2 uses the same step again for Distance.
- A retreat at capped Distance should set `movement.retreatedAtMax` and leave the foot number unchanged unless the opponent advances with the retreater; the renderer shows this as a hop-step out and recovery.
- The RPS triangle is Light tags Movement, Movement dodges Heavy, Heavy stuffs Light.
- All attacks deal 2 base damage. Stored damage spends on the next landed hit, carries through one following exchange, and expires in `completeActiveExchange` if unspent.
- Hit streaks add immediate combo damage: +2 on the second landed hit in a row, +4 on the third. Dodge streaks add +4 combo stored damage on the second consecutive dodge.
- Add or update `scenarios.json` whenever range, stored damage, combo damage, pressure, attack commitment, or post-hit positioning changes.
- `wireframe-renderer.js` owns the fighter skeleton; use the spine/neck chain there to keep head, neck, chest, and pelvis attached across poses.
- Body animation starts from the shared setup pose in `wireframe-renderer.js`; every attack pose must blend back to neutral by the end of its action, while Flying Knee may still advance the projected foot position after impact.
- `index.html` should only translate game events into draw parameters; do not duplicate combat legality there. The scrolling number line is presentation for projected `footLine` and attack foot positions.
- Run `node scripts/test-game.mjs ./neon-ring-tag --all --seeds 42,1337` after rules changes.

## Animation guidance

- Keep combat truth in `game.js` and animation truth in the renderer. The renderer may overlap actions, exaggerate contact, or add recovery, but it should only dramatize resolved events from projection.
- Use stable anchors. Front-foot numbers are the spatial source of truth, and overhead combat text is anchored to nameplate positions rather than animated heads or roots. Motion can travel during a strike, but it should settle back to the resolved foot state.
- Grounding must only consider rendered skeleton joints. Helper vectors such as `strikeShinFollow`, poles, target offsets, or other pose metadata are not body parts and must not participate in floor clamps or bounds checks, or they can lift the entire fighter off the ground.
- Treat `actionTimeline()` as presentation timing, not rules timing. Rules resolve movement first, but movement-caused whiffs read better when the attack and evasive movement animate at the same time.
- Build each technique from a shared stance plus explicit phases: load, chamber, drive, contact, and recovery. Shared helpers are good for math, blending, constraints, and drawing; the actual pose blocks should stay direct enough that a designer can tune body mechanics by reading the numbers.
- Make strikes show their real mechanics. A cross needs a small front-foot step, dropped center of gravity, hip and torso drive, rear hand contact, and a retraction back to stance. A teep needs knee chamber first, hip thrust second, and only partial target blending so the leg does not collapse. A knee from clinch needs its target blend faded during recovery so the leg settles smoothly.
- Do not swap limb layers during a strike. Far limbs stay in far containers and near limbs stay in near containers; if depth needs emphasis, adjust fighter-level ordering instead. Sudden limb z-index changes look like body parts popping through the model.
- Keep the skeleton visually consistent. Wireframe path strokes use round caps and joins, and hands, feet, hips, torso, spine, and neck should share the same rounded language.
- Damage, miss, dodge, and stored-damage text should behave like HUD feedback, not debris. It should hold long enough to read, drift only a little, and stay near the relevant fighter's stable overhead plate.
- Visual QA should target specific exchanges. Force deterministic cross, teep, knee, movement-whiff, hit, miss, and dodge cases, then scrub local animation progress around chamber, contact, and recovery instead of judging only from live play speed.
- Abstract helpers around repeated invariants, not around martial-arts intent. If a helper hides whether a limb is chambering, driving, or recovering, it will make future animation fixes slower and less realistic.

## Realistic strike animation rules

- Never stretch limbs or the torso to make contact. Upper arm, forearm, thigh, shin, pelvis-to-chest, chest-to-neck, neck-to-head, shoulder-bar, and hip-bar proportions must stay at the body model lengths. Do not add move-specific thigh or shin lengths just to make a strike land; teeps, knees, and flying knees must all solve through the fixed strike leg lengths. If a strike cannot reach, solve distance with footwork first, then hip and shoulder rotation, then limited target blending inside the real reach envelope.
- Manage distance through technique mechanics, not root sliding. For the cross, the front foot may step only as far as needed while the rear foot stays planted; if the fist already reaches the chin without a step, do not force a step. Other strikes should use their natural travel: teep hip thrust, knee lift, flying-knee jump, or clinch compression.
- Sequence upper-body power from the ground up. A convincing punch loads, turns the hips toward the opponent first, then turns the shoulder bar, then launches the fist late. The fist should accelerate into contact, with peak speed at the end of the punch, instead of drifting forward at a constant speed.
- For leg attacks, use shoulder-to-hip counter-rotation to create the whip. First rotate the shoulder bar on the striking-leg side toward the opponent; then, as the kick or knee launches, rotate that shoulder side back the way it came while the hip on the attacking-leg side rotates toward the opponent. The opposing shoulder/hip timing should make the striking leg whip forward without changing thigh or shin length.
- Straight knees should not levitate the fighter. Shift weight onto the front foot, keep that support foot planted, chamber the rear knee with the rear foot tucked slightly behind and below the knee, then thrust the rear hip forward/up through the target. At contact the knee point should be high and sharp, the shin should hang down/back from the knee, the rear foot should point downward after chamber, the torso should arch back behind the driving hip, and the support side can rise visually onto the ball of the foot while the fighter's root stays grounded.
- Flying knees may jump, but the jump supports the knee drive instead of carrying the whole fighter upward like a hover. Launch off the lead foot, chamber the rear knee, keep the rear foot tucked behind the knee, and use forward travel plus hip thrust more than vertical lift.
- Knee leg motion should be continuous, not solver-swapped. Use one blended leg amount for neutral -> chamber/contact -> neutral, blend thigh/shin lengths only within model limits, fade foot angles before neutral, and keep the shin-follow vector mostly vertical-down with only a small rear tuck so the shin hangs under the knee instead of lining up with the thigh.
- Separate the body phases. Use independent phase curves for load, hip turn, shoulder turn, whip/contact, and recovery. The hips and shoulders may rotate while the hand is still chambered; most hand travel should come from the late whip phase.
- Keep the punch straight. Use an arm pole target to keep the elbow below the shoulder line and on the tucked side during extension, then let IK solve the elbow without flipping. A cross should read as a long straight punch, not an elbow or a looping hook.
- Target anatomy, not generic centers. The cross aims at the opponent's near-side chin point, slightly below and closer than the head-center joint, and the fist should only slightly overlap that point. Keep a small contact gap before the target so the hand touches instead of burying through the skull.
- Use the same attack pose for hit, whiff, and range cases. The strike mechanics should not change because the combat result changed; only target blending, impact reactions, and defender behavior should differ. A miss from distance is the same cross falling short, not a different punch.
- Blend targets after body constraints and before final limb IK. First constrain the body frame, attach shoulders and hips, re-constrain the body, blend the fist toward the contact target within max reach, set arm poles, then run limb IK. This preserves anatomy while still letting contact look intentional.
- Make recovery visible but not mushy. Snap into contact, then retract and blend back to the shared neutral stance by the end of the action. Recovery can be slower than launch, but it should not drag the contact frame backward before the strike visibly lands.
- Verify with numbers and screenshots. Sample joint distances at chamber/contact/recovery to prove limb lengths are unchanged, check fist speed rises into contact, and render deterministic screenshots at the contact frame to catch elbow flips, body stretching, or over-penetration.
